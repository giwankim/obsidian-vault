## 1 - Have fun & tinker with small projects.

Pick any of these as a starting point … then graduate to some reference materials, books… Keep continuously learning via conferences, blogs, articles etc…

## Three tracks:

- \[OFL\] Offline Rendering - What’s used in movies! Non-real-time, we have a lot of time to generate an image. Theory of lights, scattering, materials. Monte-carlo solutions of the rendering equation: Ray tracing, Path tracing.
- \[RTR\] Real-Time Rendering - What’s used in video games! Some of the same theory as offline, but the practical implementation has to deal with GPUs, rasterization, shaders… On this track, one can start from the “low level” and learn a 3D API, shaders etc, writing a renderer from scratch, or start from a 3D engine (e.g. Unity, Unreal…), learn higher level concepts and then graduate to understand how things work at a lower level.
- \[ART\] Fabrication, generative, digital art and so on. Can use both real-time and off-line rendering, and other techniques for procedural generation of geometry, patterns etc…

## \[OFL\] First steps:

### \[OFL\] Ray Tracing in one weekend

![](https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXch--gn_pLG1Y621uH6-euVX9-YmlA2fOWSOw_xL52gQSTSLG-eWNMKUiO--bVcyZEjzVbWFFy3IA9T4zeFb7IuLgbJJL77CVBAGj8CWFqTQGMXRD_NBhG9I5-QvV31YuwSozEXntVi9_FADCtDo3QijJlC=s800?key=GZq0DP-rEaP6dZHcpvAvkw)

A fun and free booklet by Peter Shirley who authored many other (longer) books on computer graphics. It guides through a weekend project, building a simple offline renderer. There’s nothing like raytracing to get hooked on 3d graphics programming!

[https://raytracing.github.io/](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://raytracing.github.io/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987551203&usg=AOvVaw3BRFD5_A5_h4CGw23IfQDJ)

Take this project if you’re interested in generating photorealistic images, and want to understand the physics of light transport. From here, you can graduate both towards the intricacies of how to make path-tracing practical for complex scenes and lighting scenarios - or towards a deeper understanding of the physics of light and materials - which is relevant also for real-time (GPU, rasterization-based) rendering.

Of course, it’s impossible not to mention “PBRT” [https://www.pbrt.org/](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.pbrt.org/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987552395&usg=AOvVaw0zYkjPzNhNPUxRbwpzeBd-) here… and we will, but in the “reading material” section below - as I feel like PBRT goes beyond “tinkering” - it requires some serious commitment!

![](https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXctZZizmL_w-OttEDewtpJbHd0WJ0yi_A4iK9wVAv9oNyR-8Q0xpk58RSPfbpMhW0ReJiMpMySLuVOeqCkDaph_uufAhmS70Gc1N7kaemn7LUEodc2QceyV3PlSUZ08Po0ILFReLJwLoOOTBNi1q4NkcFUN6gfhpt81k7K26bX2bbk=s800?key=GZq0DP-rEaP6dZHcpvAvkw)

Related: Tinker with an educational path tracer.

SmallPT is a tiny program (99 lines of C++ code!) which generates photorealistic images. Another great way of getting started with offline rendering! [http://www.kevinbeason.com/smallpt/](https://www.google.com/url?q=http://www.kevinbeason.com/smallpt/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987553339&usg=AOvVaw2J2tPCujjSle-65Q8zL0wL) …before “Raytracing in one weekend” it was probably the best starting point to tinker with offline rendering.

In its “raw” form it’s not too easy to understand (but fun to tinker with), but on the homepage there is a slide deck by David Cline that does a great job at explaining each single line. [https://drive.google.com/file/d/0B8g97JkuSSBwUENiWTJXeGtTOHFmSm51UC01YWtCZw/view](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://drive.google.com/file/d/0B8g97JkuSSBwUENiWTJXeGtTOHFmSm51UC01YWtCZw/view&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987554069&usg=AOvVaw16uSx6iIm6cmWkE-0sgh3q)

There are a few other path tracers that are similarly made with simplicity in mind. For example: MiniLight [https://www.hxa.name/minilight/](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.hxa.name/minilight/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987554460&usg=AOvVaw3M_GE2ixktexJQKn_FI4hw), [http://www.smallvcm.com/](https://www.google.com/url?q=http://www.smallvcm.com/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987554612&usg=AOvVaw3oc_pc51VMo9aSSyPdznMV) (which implements a quite advanced light transport algorithm - thus - not the easiest to play with) and Nori [https://www.cs.cornell.edu/courses/cs6630/2012sp/nori/](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.cs.cornell.edu/courses/cs6630/2012sp/nori/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987554925&usg=AOvVaw0d9710AkCHBpIIQMjk0hZG) \- [http://rgl.epfl.ch/courses/ACG17](https://www.google.com/url?q=http://rgl.epfl.ch/courses/ACG17&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987555057&usg=AOvVaw179eG5nlf8LPAy1n7h7Y7Z)

### \[OFL - mostly\] Scratchapixel.

Part beginner's textbook, part guide for practical exercises, Scratchapixel has some truly great content.

The downside of this website is that it's still work-in-progress, and certain chapters are not as good as others.

Take this path if you want a bit more of a structured and theoretical introduction to the topic - more akin to reading a book. You’ll probably want to write your own small project along the way, and use the resources in “continuous learning” to fill gaps.

Focuses on offline rendering, but the theoretical basis are the same for real-time… [https://www.scratchapixel.com](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.scratchapixel.com&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987556626&usg=AOvVaw1i2k0ntWw8vu05NhhvTu83)

## \[RTR\] First steps:

### \[RTR\] Play with WebGl2!

![](https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXcLpo4tV3tDnzcdFjqfrwQ7tFo_jTvDjli_pSKL1Tr-Tkxml-Voaei_t1D7SW-0rjY5zGBSRLJ1CC8e__ZBr_CzOwYde1VbzSI5fQskIvSRV0KLHSONZf80Pv2momVy5kor4cf5rx6G1_95sq8aWOiDJg=s800?key=GZq0DP-rEaP6dZHcpvAvkw)

Sadly perhaps, WebGl is today one of the few more “beginner friendly” APIs that are not discontinued…

This interactive web book provides a good introduction and all the code is tinkerable! [https://webgl2fundamentals.org/](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://webgl2fundamentals.org/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987557813&usg=AOvVaw0ZazIi4pQAjF8eLAofEFpP)

Take this path if you want to understand how GPUs are used, the lower-level details of images and 3d objects, and how to build your own real-time 3d engine. This is often done by people who are interested in videogame programming, but real-time rendering is also important for interactive/responsive visualization in general.

Typically, this path leads eventually to C/C++ and using modern graphics APIs such as Vulkan and DirectX 12 - to get the maximum performance.

Alternatives:

[http://www.webglacademy.com/](https://www.google.com/url?q=http://www.webglacademy.com/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987559000&usg=AOvVaw1JEt0MiceNQO0vKlGtfnoD) is very good and interactive, it also goes into some advanced topics.

[https://github.com/stackgl/shader-school](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://github.com/stackgl/shader-school&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987559344&usg=AOvVaw1WSrATJCXTNktkMgR4PPWa)

[http://learnwebgl.brown37.net/](https://www.google.com/url?q=http://learnwebgl.brown37.net/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987559568&usg=AOvVaw35ZufaaeZl6xlYGlWpwyuM)

I would not necessarily use WebGL directly btw - there are wrappers that make programming it much more pleasant.

PicoGL (a WebGL2 wrapper) is amazing. It’s truly a nice API to use, but to my knowledge there are no tutorials that use that wrapper [https://tsherif.github.io/picogl.js/](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://tsherif.github.io/picogl.js/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987560239&usg=AOvVaw3ckF57YgIsxwROfRUDrMDl) \- seems a bit abandoned, but not everything requires continuous improvement. I imagine most web graphics fervor these days is around webGPU.

Alternatives: SwissGL is also interesting [https://github.com/google/swissgl](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://github.com/google/swissgl&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987560744&usg=AOvVaw0tedJuMcq2RD5KuzUaqxqz) and the same is for twgl [https://github.com/greggman/twgl.js](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://github.com/greggman/twgl.js&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987560907&usg=AOvVaw1_MJw0W1hcJt0ybHAzdIFP) and regl [http://regl.party/](https://www.google.com/url?q=http://regl.party/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987561085&usg=AOvVaw1dHuJqaylPvS7MXPgoAUoJ) \- all WebGL2, but I prefer picogl to these.

### \[RTR\] Three.js

![](https://lh7-rt.googleusercontent.com/docsz/AD_4nXcKSBQnmTUSw5Q6Uj7TlXT__k7CA4f6EXa80XCH73m4-ZWpEc_oqxOdfE9-txzeB-Ko6B_pMSfzYfr9lFvy-_0Q3K3ykidVOqyJTHY7ysfyryDp6cV1vqYDrRxfSVQ9yPdFXgcTXvXM-ZZ7Auoe5ueShQ=s800?key=GZq0DP-rEaP6dZHcpvAvkw)

If WebGL is a good starting point to learn a GPU 3d graphics API today, three.js is a great first 3d engine to begin with.

This means it’s more of a high-level introduction than working with a GPU api, directly working with scenes, objects, lights, textures etc… [https://threejs.org/docs/#manual/en/introduction/Useful-links](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://threejs.org/docs/%23manual/en/introduction/Useful-links&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987562316&usg=AOvVaw2OZ9nlJBXYQ95i_pZ6T02e)

After this, one can graduate to engines like Unity, Unreal or Godot, or learn a 3d API and write an engine from scratch…

Interactive 3d Graphics, an udacity course from Eric Haines (one of the authors of Real-Time Rendering) goes through the basics using WebGL and three.js.The course is a bit lengthy: [https://www.udacity.com/course/interactive-3d-graphics--cs291](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.udacity.com/course/interactive-3d-graphics--cs291&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987563136&usg=AOvVaw1SplNCdBTcumx28KD_SxAv)

The three.js website also has its own list of resources, and the documentation itself is quite nice [https://threejs.org/manual/#en/fundamentals](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://threejs.org/manual/%23en/fundamentals&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987563708&usg=AOvVaw0d7qYYafHGSI1GR3ZSmg01)

The “book of shaders” is truly a marvel, an interactive online book going through the basics of pixel shaders and showing how to make art with them.

[http://thebookofshaders.com/](https://www.google.com/url?q=http://thebookofshaders.com/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987570981&usg=AOvVaw0p_7mqVja9xFog1Jt1eubZ)

It’s not a full introduction to 3d real-time rendering as it only covers pixel shaders to directly generate images, no 3d geometry involved, but it’s a good read to familiarise with the shading language that is used for real-time rendering.

Once you’re done, you can graduate to ShaderToy. Shadertoy is the best way to start tinkering with GPU programming. Made by Inigo Quilez and Pol Jeremias, masters of procedural 3d graphics, it quickly grew into a vibrant community of shader authors.

As for “the book of shaders” here shaders are directly used to generate procedural images, without 3d meshes (geometry). This is not how 3d real-time rendering usually works, but it is a great introduction to shader and GPU programming.

One really great introduction is this YouTube video by kishimisu [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f4s1h2YETNY](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v%3Df4s1h2YETNY&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987572609&usg=AOvVaw3msgyG3RhJaVyW1jdS8w1I)

See also: [https://gamedevelopment.tutsplus.com/tutorials/a-beginners-guide-to-coding-graphics-shaders--cms-23313](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://gamedevelopment.tutsplus.com/tutorials/a-beginners-guide-to-coding-graphics-shaders--cms-23313&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987572971&usg=AOvVaw2WUWfa66mTJFyuxab9H71t) [http://pixelshaders.com/](https://www.google.com/url?q=http://pixelshaders.com/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987573084&usg=AOvVaw1gm7kPZtKHIcGU9UIuQyxG)

When you feel confident of the basics, Inigo’s website is a treasure trove of advanced procedural shader stuff! [http://www.iquilezles.org/www/articles/raymarchingdf/raymarchingdf.htm](https://www.google.com/url?q=http://www.iquilezles.org/www/articles/raymarchingdf/raymarchingdf.htm&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987573540&usg=AOvVaw2RWv3Q3Mq7TMsilGKyiSQM)

## Books

\[RTR\] Real-Time Rendering.

Probably the most well-known “encyclopaedic” book for 3D real-time rendering.

It is theoretical, but not hard to read. It’s great both as a reference text and to read end-to-end.

It will not guide you through practical projects though, and it will not delve deeply into any subject, it’s meant to provide the necessary background to understand more advanced papers and presentations.

\[RTR, OFF\] Game Math (free, online).

[https://gamemath.com/book/](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://gamemath.com/book/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987607078&usg=AOvVaw0tmhWdXXfEJXBShwSfYg4j)

\[RTR, OFF\] Graphics Codex.

This is another great reference text, very well done. In contrast to Real-Time Rendering, this has an offline rendering slant, but lots of the underlying math and physics are relevant to real-time computer graphics as well.

The author keeps also a page of small projects and exercises on [http://graphicscodex.com/projects/projects/](https://www.google.com/url?q=http://graphicscodex.com/projects/projects/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987607841&usg=AOvVaw37eDS0vg4gMoIXYndUk5s_)

\[OFL\] Physically based rendering: from theory to implementation.

[https://www.pbr-book.org/](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.pbr-book.org/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987608260&usg=AOvVaw2zZ3wzRFEkIxdDJdNJHpzc) Known as “ PBRT ”, this one is not about real-time rendering but offline path tracing.

Most of the theory applies to physically based shading in games as well, and the big perk of this book is that it’s written as “literate programming”, the book is itself the source-code of a raytracer. It is not a quick read, but by the end of it you’ll have a nice photorealistic renderer!

After PBRT, you might be ready to have a look at “research” oriented path tracers like Mitsuba [https://www.mitsuba-renderer.org/](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.mitsuba-renderer.org/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987609250&usg=AOvVaw1iD_g5FPENKfi5Y6UV2THw) and Lightmetrica [http://lightmetrica.org/](https://www.google.com/url?q=http://lightmetrica.org/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987609451&usg=AOvVaw1wfS7LRXoDgHgYqhXfQajC)

\[RTR, OFF\] Computer Graphics, Principles and Practice.

Another very notable “encyclopaedic” book, tends to be a bit more theoretical than RTR, but it’s a great read…

\[RTR\] Game Engine Architecture.

One of the very few books written by an industry professional (Real-Time Collision Detection is another example, as the book below).

Recommended. Covers also workflows, debugging, using visual studio etc. [https://www.gameenginebook.com/](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.gameenginebook.com/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987610603&usg=AOvVaw3bt_nlYOrUc8FpKJgC2eTK)

\[RTR\] Practical Rendering & Computation with Direct3d 11.

This book guides through Direct3D 11 and it’s written by industry experts (Pettineo is the rendering lead at Ready at Dawn).

It also comes with / builds a 3d rendering engine.

The only downside is that it might be a bit overly specific delving into lots of details specific to DX11.

\[RTR\] Mastering Graphics Programming with Vulkan. This book is a must have. It does assume familiarity with Vulkan as a prerequisite and from there builds a fairly modern (albeit still “educational”) engine around it! [https://www.packtpub.com/product/mastering-graphics-programming-with-vulkan/9781803244792](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.packtpub.com/product/mastering-graphics-programming-with-vulkan/9781803244792&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987612497&usg=AOvVaw0mb3H-0-2AFu8BludOFgZM)

Eric Lengyel’s books.

[http://www.terathon.com/lengyel/](https://www.google.com/url?q=http://www.terathon.com/lengyel/&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1770855987612895&usg=AOvVaw20ixJ6a7YQ-ZKb4EhNo4Fs) He has been recently working on a series of books on the fundamentals of 3d engines.

His writings are known for their precision and quality!

### Honourable mentions

These are also important resources for beginners, but I’ve created a separate section because the contents might only partially apply.

Real-Time Collision Detection.

This book, as the title implies, is not related to Computer Graphics.

Nonetheless it’s -really- well written and it’s more general than it might seem. The first few chapters go through essential math in a very clear way, while end ending talks competently about optimizations and numerical issues. Also, most of the spatial subdivision structures discussed also apply to CG (visibility).

\[RTR\] Game Programming Patterns.

Chapter 6 only, it explains the basics of modern CPU performance quite clearly. For the rest, I do not endorse design patterns. Especially avoid chapter 2.
